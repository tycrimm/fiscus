Variation A

Inline split — every category gets its own row

UNCALLED splits into SBS (personal coinvest) and FUND (SHV / other commitments). LIABILITIES splits into DUE SOON (revolving / credit card, 0–30 days) and AMORTIZED (term debt — car loan, mortgage). No nesting — every line is a sibling. Trades visual hierarchy for direct comparability.

Notes

  • Pros: direct comparability — DUE SOON ($3K) sits next to AMORTIZED ($171K) with no parent-total to skip past. SBS vs fund commitments are visible at a glance.
  • Cons: 6 rows is a lot for a glance. Loses the "liabilities total" you had before — you have to add DUE SOON + AMORTIZED in your head.
  • When this wins: if the tenor / commitment-type split is information you want every time you look at the page.

Variation C

Annotation — keep one row, breakdown in a caption

UNCALLED and LIABILITIES stay as single rows showing the total. A tiny secondary line underneath spells out the split in shorthand ("$12K SBS · $3K fund", "$3K due 30d · $171K amortized"). Minimum row count, breakdown still visible.

Variation D

Urgency grouping — re-cluster by time-to-cash

Re-organizes around the question "what cash do I need soon?" — UNCALLED (capital calls, lumpy & unpredictable) and DUE SOON (credit cards, must pay this month) cluster together as OWED SOON. AMORTIZED (car loan, mortgage — predictable monthly bite) sits alone. Most opinionated: it stops mirroring the schema and groups by behavior.

Notes

  • Pros: answers the cash-management question directly — OWED SOON sums to ~$18K, that's what your liquid needs to cover at any moment. AMORTIZED is just a monthly-bite tracker.
  • Cons: conflates two structurally different things — capital calls (asset commitments, eventually become PRIVATE) and credit cards (pure debt, no offsetting asset). The grouping is behavioral, not accounting-clean.
  • When this wins: if the rail's primary job is "do I have cash?" rather than "what's my balance sheet?". More useful for active cash management, less useful for net-worth tracking.
